4  The calibrated-Bayes lens

NoteGuardrails binding this chapter

This is the heaviest chapter, and it is the one where the line between borrowed prior art and this review’s own contribution is most easily blurred. Each guardrail below is stated as a precondition on who owns what: the principle, the mechanism, the substrate, and this review’s exact result are pinned to their correct sources before any prose advances.

  • G-24 (the calibrated-Bayes principle is prior art). The principle — judge a Bayesian model against frequentist operating characteristics, preserve the inference center, realign the covariance — is established in Rubin (1984) and Little (2012). The program claims only its first application to the meta-analytic RVE covariance target, never the principle and never the “calibrated Bayes” label.
  • G-21 (the center is the posterior mean). The affine-recoloring fixed point is the posterior / Monte-Carlo mean, never the posterior mode (MAP). Mode-anchored near-neighbors are named as contrasts, not as the mechanism.
  • G-01 / G-02 (Savitsky & Williams are survey-scoped). Williams & Savitsky (2021) and Savitsky & Williams (2022) work in survey inference and never mention meta-analysis; their projection double-weights both the unit-level likelihood and the random-effect (group) prior, and is approximate/asymptotic. The survey-to-meta-analysis leg is this review’s own extension.
  • G-08 (W&S asymptotic vs. this review’s exact finite-sample). The Williams & Savitsky scale-and-shape projection is proven asymptotically — an approximation; the exact finite-sample unit-weight-boundary identity is this review’s own, distinct in kind, and never stood in for by the asymptotic mechanism.
  • G-25 (the Godambe substrate motivates; it does not solve). The composite-likelihood / estimating-equation substrate (Binder 1983; Varin et al. 2008, 2011) establishes why a covariance target is well-defined under misspecification; it never guarantees, eliminates, or solves the dependent-effect correction.
  • G-16 (the unit-weight boundary is an exact contribution). The unit-weight boundary is the configuration at which calibration is mathematically exact — this review’s result — framed as a precondition for a stated theorem scope, never a limitation, approximation, or restriction adopted for tractability.
  • G-17 / G-18 (the Theorem scopes are distinct). The conditional identity holds over the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\), never arbitrary heteroscedastic or arbitrary positive-definite \(V_j\); the marginal identity is strictly more general, resting only on a matched marginal Gaussian kernel. The two scopes are never flattened to one.
  • G-06 (CCC is this review’s coinage). “CCC” (Cholesky Calibration Correction) is this review’s own name for the meta-analytic operationalization of the Williams & Savitsky mechanism. CCC implements that mechanism; it is never “from,” “introduced by,” or “due to” Savitsky or Williams, and never a “known survey technique.”
  • G-10 / G-11 (blockwise by design; estimand justifies the marginal target). CCC is blockwise by design because the full-joint \((\beta,\theta)\) sandwich is structurally rank-deficient — a principled design choice, not a failure; the marginal target is justified separately by the estimand. Rank and estimand arguments are kept apart.
  • G-19 / G-20 (\(\rho\) is conditional, not the marginal ICC). \(\rho\) is the conditional Level-1 residual working correlation of \(y_j\) given \((\beta,\theta_j)\), not the marginal intra-class correlation; fixed-\(\rho\) / equicorrelation is a reporting-scope choice, not the site at which exactness arises.
  • G-09 (covariance-matching is not coverage). Covariance-matching is exact, finite-sample, and algebraic; coverage is asymptotic in \(J\) and simulation-shown. The two are never conflated; the affirmative matching-versus-coverage statement is hosted at Chapter 6, not here.

The formal theorems are cited motivationally only and are never re-proved here: they appear as prose cross-references to companion manuscripts (in preparation), not as @-citations, because those manuscripts have no bibliography entries. The verbatim negative-existence sentence is single-hosted at §3.5 of the previous chapter and is never restated here; this chapter carries only its positioning counterpart.

The previous chapter documented an absence: across the published breadth of Bayesian meta-analysis, no method targets the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance as an explicit calibration target, and the absence is one of application, not of Bayesian capacity. What is missing is not machinery but a warrant — a principled reason a Bayesian analyst may import a frequentist covariance target into a posterior workflow without abandoning the posterior as the inferential object. This chapter supplies that warrant and, in doing so, draws a careful map of who owns each part of it. It assembles, in order, the license that makes the import principled (Little/Rubin, prior art), the mechanism that executes it (Williams & Savitsky’s survey-scoped Cholesky projection), the mathematical substrate that makes a covariance target well-defined under misspecification (the Godambe / composite-likelihood sandwich), the void that substrate opens but cannot fill, this review’s own exact result that fills it at the unit-weight boundary, and the positioning that closes the gap. Throughout, the load-bearing object is named each time it appears — the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance, over the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\) — and at every seam the line between borrowed motivation and owned contribution is held: the principle, the mechanism, and the substrate are prior art; the meta-analytic application, the exact finite-sample identity, and the “CCC” name are this review’s.

4.1 4.1 The principle is prior art; only its meta-analytic application is this review’s

The first and most important act of attribution is to credit the licensing principle to its sources. The calibrated-Bayes principle — that a Bayesian model is judged against frequentist operating characteristics, preserving the inference center while realigning the covariance — is established prior art in Rubin (1984) and Little (2012), and this review claims neither the principle nor the label that names it (CL-P3-01, CONDITIONAL; G-24). Rubin (1984) supplies the calibration definition and the operating-characteristic machinery — the \(\mathrm{PC}(X)\) apparatus by which a Bayesian procedure is evaluated against its repeated-sampling behavior — and frames the calibrated Bayesian as one whose posterior statements are checked against frequentist performance. Little (2004, 2012) carries the same logic into the survey and official-statistics context, framing Bayesian inference as judged against design-based frequentist operating characteristics, and explicitly crediting the lineage upstream to Box (1980) and Rubin (1984). The principle is thus doubly anchored as prior art: it is established in named sources, and those sources themselves locate it earlier still. The program’s contribution is the application of this principle to one specific object — the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance in meta-analysis — not the principle that licenses the application.

The principle is live prior art, invoked repeatedly in adjacent work, and each downstream invocation confirms it without relocating its credit. Bissiri, Holmes and Walker (2016) calibrate a learning rate so that posterior quantiles align with frequentist intervals, supplying the general Gibbs-Bayes machinery that permits replacing a likelihood target with a loss or estimating-equation target tied to frequentist performance. Frazier, Drovandi and Kohn (2026) operationalize the principle for a Gibbs-posterior coverage target, judging a generalized-Bayes estimator by its sandwich-based frequentist behavior. Mathur (2025) and Williams, Rast and Bürkner (2018) judge Bayesian estimators of the meta-analytic mean and of heterogeneity by frequentist coverage and bias. These are genuine instances of the calibrated-Bayes posture in action; yet none articulates “preserve the center, realign the covariance” for a covariance target in dependent-effect meta-analysis, and each leaves the credit for the principle with Little and Rubin. The “calibrated Bayes” label itself is coined later in that lineage, not by this review; this review inherits both the principle and its name as established vocabulary and adds only the destination it points the principle at.

4.2 4.2 The affine recoloring: preserve the mean, realign the covariance

Under that license, the operation this review needs takes a precise form: an affine recoloring of the posterior draws that leaves the inference center fixed and realigns the marginal covariance toward a frequentist target. The center it preserves is the posterior (Monte-Carlo) mean, and the realignment is toward a named sandwich covariance; this mechanism is motivated by the calibrated-Bayes principle and is the reason covariance-target adoption is justifiable inside a Bayesian workflow at all (CL-P3-02, HEURISTIC; G-21, G-24). The preserve-the-mean half is attested by Little (2004, 2012), whose posterior mean recovers the design-weighted estimator and is the quantity held fixed. The affine-realignment half is attested mechanically by Williams & Savitsky (2021, Eq. 13), whose scale-and-shape map recenters the draws and rescales their spread toward a frequentist sandwich target. The two halves together describe the move: a linear map applied to centered draws, with the mean as the fixed point of the map and the covariance as the object realigned.

The fixed point is the mean, and that choice is load-bearing: an affine map preserves the center only when the centering point is the mean, so the mechanism is mean-anchored by construction, never mode-anchored. The distinction disqualifies an otherwise close relative. Shaby (2014)’s “open-faced sandwich” recoloring is an affine map of the same algebraic family; it realigns a quasi-posterior covariance to a Godambe sandwich while recentering the draws, but anchors at the quasi-Bayes point estimate, the mode of the composite criterion, an asymptotic-sampling device rather than the posterior mean. It is therefore a mode-anchored near-neighbor that this review does not borrow as its mechanism: the calibrated-Bayes center this review preserves is the posterior mean, and the MAP is reserved for an entirely separate likelihood-identity consequence. Naming the anchor correctly keeps the mechanism faithful to the Little–Rubin license, under which the preserved center is the quantity whose frequentist operating characteristics are being calibrated. All such carriers are asymptotic and survey- or generalized-Bayes-scoped; none instantiates the exact finite-sample, mean-anchored identity that §4.5 reserves to this review. The recoloring as a principle is prior art; its exact finite-sample realization is not.

4.3 4.3 The survey-to-Bayes bridge is W&S’s; the meta-analytic leg is this review’s

The mechanism named in §4.2 has a precise origin, and crediting it correctly means holding it to its scope without letting it travel further than its authors took it — in survey inference, not meta-analysis. Williams & Savitsky (2021) and Savitsky & Williams (2022) established the survey-estimation-to-Bayesian-paradigm bridge through a Cholesky scale-and-shape projection that double-weights both the unit-level likelihood and the random-effect (group) prior; they never mention meta-analysis, and their projection is approximate and asymptotic (CL-P3-03, CONDITIONAL; G-01, G-02, G-07, G-08). Both weight levels must be stated, because the double-weighting is the construction’s defining feature and the discipline that prevents borrowing it as a single-level finite-sample tool. Williams & Savitsky (2021) double-weight the unit pseudo-likelihood, with weights inversely proportional to the inclusion probability, and the PSU-block / group-level resampling. Savitsky & Williams (2022) carry the same weight-exponentiated construction at both the unit-likelihood and the group-prior levels, while explicitly deferring the covariance fix itself to Williams & Savitsky (2021). The bridge is survey machinery at both levels.

The bridge is asymptotic, not finite-sample; its hardest case is shown by simulation rather than proved. Williams & Savitsky (2021) establish the scale-and-shape projection as a large-sample design-theory result, and the clustering-driven rescaling case — the configuration nearest the dependent-effect setting — is demonstrated by their own simulation evidence (the DE5 and PPS3 designs), not by a theorem. A broad survey-side substrate corroborates the projection’s provenance and asymptotic character without touching meta-analysis: the pseudo-posterior foundation of Savitsky & Toth (2016), with survey weights raised into the likelihood exponent and only an asymptotic contraction proved; the informative-sampling and weighted-distribution lineage of Pfeffermann, Krieger and Rinott (1998); and the survey-software realization of Hornby, Williams, Savitsky and Elkasabi (2023), in which the Cholesky square root appears only as an implementation option, not as a named correction. Each is survey inference, asymptotic, and meta-analysis-free. The survey-to-meta-analysis leg is therefore not something Savitsky & Williams did — it is this review’s own extension — and the bridge is cited here strictly for mechanism motivation: it tells this review what map to apply, not that the map is exact, and not that anyone has applied it to dependent effect sizes. The program, not Savitsky & Williams, owns finite-sample exactness; their work is the asymptotic mechanism, never a benchmark for this review’s finite-sample claim.

4.4 4.4 The Godambe substrate names the void; composite likelihood does not fill it

A license and a mechanism still leave one question open: under model misspecification, why is a covariance target even well-defined? The answer is the Godambe / estimating-equation substrate, and its credit, too, must be assigned precisely: it explains why a target exists, not that a correction already does. The Godambe sandwich and estimating-equation substrate supplies the asymptotic mathematical justification for treating covariance realignment under misspecification as principled rather than ad hoc (CL-P3-04, HEURISTIC; G-25). Binder (1983) is the survey origin of the design-based estimating-equation sandwich, deriving the robust variance for estimating-equation parameters under a working model that may misspecify structure. Varin (2008) and Varin, Reid and Firth (2011) formalize the composite-likelihood case, defining the Godambe information \(G = H J^{-1} H\) as the correct information matrix when a composite or pseudo-likelihood model is misspecified, and naming \(G^{-1}\) the robust variance, with Varin, Reid and Firth (2011) cautioning that the robust variance “does not guarantee high efficiency.” This substrate establishes why a covariance target is well-defined under misspecification — why realigning a misspecified working model’s covariance toward a sandwich is a principled move and not an arbitrary one. It is cited to license the existence of a target, never as evidence that a correction for dependent meta-analytic effects has already been constructed.

The substrate’s reach stops well short of supplying that correction (CL-P3-05, CONDITIONAL; G-25). Composite likelihood is asymptotic and assumes estimable low-dimensional margins; under unknown within-study correlations — the regime dependent-effect meta-analysis actually inhabits — its operating guarantees degrade, so it motivates, but does not by itself supply, a covariance correction for dependent effects. Varin, Reid and Firth (2011) are explicit that asymptotic normality with \(G^{-1}\) holds for the within-cluster size \(n\) large with the number of clusters \(m\) fixed, that efficiency is application-specific and can be poor, and that consistency under increasing \(m\) is not rigorously established. The generalized-Bayes literature reinforces the same boundary from the other side: Lyddon, Holmes and Walker (2019) show that under misspecification the model-based posterior spread is the wrong asymptotic object and the sandwich is the right one, correcting a scalar loss-scale by information matching rather than realigning a covariance to a named meta-analytic target; and Tanaka (2026) shows that a scalar rate cannot in general match a sandwich shape, so the curvature-versus-sandwich gap remains open for any matrix-valued target. Each of these names the deficiency the substrate exposes; none supplies the fix. The substrate names the void; it does not fill it. What fills it must be exact, finite-sample, and meta-analytic, and that is this review’s own result.

4.5 4.5 The unit-weight boundary: this review’s exact finite-sample identity

The void the substrate opens is filled by this review’s own exact result, cited here motivationally and never re-proved. Here the attribution map turns from borrowed to owned (CL-P3-06, THEOREM; G-08, G-16, G-17). At the unit-weight boundary — where the unit-level weight \(w_{ij} = 1\) and the random-effect (group) weight \(w_\theta = 1\) — a posterior affinely recolored by the marginal RVE sandwich achieves an exact finite-sample identity between the posterior’s marginal covariance and the RVE target, over the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\). The companion manuscripts pin this result precisely, and their numbering must not be conflated. A companion manuscript (Theorems 1–2 and Corollary 1) identifies the unit-weight bridge, the marginal fixed-effect sandwich target, and the center-preserving recoloring; a companion technical report (Theorems 1–2) supplies the generic CCC center-preservation and the exact covariance-matching algebra. Both are cross-references to companion manuscripts, not bibliography entries, and the chapter motivates the method without re-deriving it.

Three scope facts make this the chapter’s hinge, each a precondition, not a concession. First, the unit-weight boundary is the exact theoretical result and this review’s contribution, the configuration at which calibration is mathematically exact, not a limitation imposed for tractability; the nonuniform-weight case is scoped-out future extension, framed as a precondition for the stated theorem scope rather than as an apology (G-16). Second, its scope is the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\) with arbitrary per-cluster scale \(a_j > 0\), never arbitrary heteroscedastic or arbitrary positive-definite \(V_j\), which is explicitly outside the proven scope (G-17). Third, this finite-sample exactness is distinct in kind from the asymptotic Williams & Savitsky mechanism: theirs is a large-sample projection, whereas this review’s unit-weight result is an algebraic equality that holds at any sample size within scope, so the asymptotic mechanism must never stand in for this review’s exactness (G-08).

Published near-neighbors sharpen the boundary by lacking it. Williams & Savitsky (2021) supply only the asymptotic scale-and-shape mechanism and state explicitly, in their own §7, that the projection does not force the pseudo-posterior variance to equal the pseudo-MLE’s in small-to-moderate samples, the asymptotic disclaimer that this review’s finite-sample identity answers, and the reason a mechanism that declines the small-sample claim cannot pre-empt it. Lee (2026) achieves, for a single survey, a design-consistent empirical sandwich match through a Cholesky affine transformation, but it is a finite-sample-imperfect match toward an empirical sandwich, not an exact finite-sample identity for the meta-analytic target. Neither occupies this review’s exact finite-sample, unit-weight-boundary result, and the contrast locates exactly what is this review’s own: not the recoloring move, not the asymptotic mechanism, but the exact finite-sample identity at the unit-weight boundary. (What this identity delivers is exact covariance-matching, an algebraic property; coverage is a separate, asymptotic-in-\(J\), simulation-shown operating characteristic, and the affirmative matching-versus-coverage statement is hosted at Chapter 6, kept in its own sentences there [G-09].)

4.6 4.6 CCC is the coinage; blockwise by design; \(\rho\) is conditional

With the exact result in hand, this review’s operationalization acquires a name, a structure, and a working-model parameter. Each is an attribution checkpoint where this review’s coinage must be separated from the borrowed mechanism. CCC (Cholesky Calibration Correction) is an original coinage for the meta-analytic operationalization of the calibrated-Bayes principle; it implements the Williams & Savitsky (2021) scale-and-shape (Cholesky) projection mechanism, with the mechanism attributed to Williams & Savitsky and the name plus the meta-analytic instantiation original to this review (CL-P3-07, POSITIONING; G-06). Williams & Savitsky (2021) supply the Cholesky \(R_2^{-1} R_1\) projection that CCC operationalizes but never use the name “CCC.” The survey-side antecedents (Savitsky & Williams (2022), Savitsky, León-Novelo and Engle (2024), and Hornby, Williams, Savitsky and Elkasabi (2023)) anchor the mechanism’s survey provenance while coining no such label and supplying no meta-analytic instantiation. CCC implements the W&S mechanism; CCC is not from, introduced by, or due to Savitsky or Williams, and it is not a known or established survey technique.

The calibration is blockwise, and this structure is a design choice grounded in geometry, not a workaround. CCC calibration is blockwise by design (a \(\beta\)-block Cholesky on the marginal sandwich target and a \(\theta\)-block diagonal correlated-effects rescaling) because the full-joint \((\beta, \theta)\) sandwich is structurally rank-deficient (rank \(\le J < K + J\), reflecting incidental-parameter geometry), so full-joint Cholesky calibration is not attempted (CL-P3-08, CONDITIONAL, PRIMARY; G-10, G-11). Rank-deficiency disqualifies the full-joint Cholesky; it does not by itself justify the marginal target. The marginal target is justified separately by the estimand: the reported quantity integrates over the random intercepts \(\theta_j\), so the reported covariance must be marginal. The two arguments are kept apart (rank-deficiency for what the full-joint cannot be, the estimand for what the reported covariance must be), and this separation is the discipline (the rank-deficiency result is established in a companion technical report, cited motivationally). Blockwise calibration is the principled realization of these two facts, not a compromise forced by either, and keeping the two apart is what prevents the contribution from reading as a tractability concession.

The working-model correlation parameter is the final attribution checkpoint, because it is the place a reader is most likely to import the wrong object. In the CCC working model, \(\rho\) is the conditional residual (Level-1) working correlation of \(y_j\) given \((\beta, \theta_j)\) — the within-cluster correlation before \(\theta_j\) is integrated out — and it is not the marginal intra-class correlation, which is inflated by \(\tau^2\) through \(\Sigma_j(\tau^2) = V_j + \tau^2\,\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top\) (CL-P3-10, CONDITIONAL, PRIMARY; G-19, G-20). The distinction is load-bearing: \(\rho\) parameterizes within-cluster Level-1 dependence, while the marginal correlation an analyst might mistake it for is the \(\tau^2\)-inflated quantity. The fixed-\(\rho\) / equicorrelation specification is a working-model and reporting-scope choice; it is not the logical site at which exactness arises, which lives instead in matching the known within-cluster covariance structure (the \(\rho\)-definition is established in a companion technical report, cited motivationally). The fixed-\(\rho\) boundary is a precondition for the stated theorem scope, never the mechanism that produces the exact identity. Framing it as scope rather than as the source of exactness keeps the §4.5 result from reading as a narrow curiosity about a fixed correlation rather than what it is: an exact match to a known covariance structure.

4.7 4.7 No prior Bayesian meta-analysis aimed the principle at the RVE/CHE sandwich

The attribution map now closes on the positioning claim. With the principle credited to Little and Rubin (§4.1), the mechanism to Williams & Savitsky (§4.3), the substrate to Binder and Varin (§4.4), and the exact identity and CCC name owned by this review (§4.5–4.6), the gap is stated: we found no prior Bayesian meta-analysis paper that has applied the calibrated-Bayes principle — preserve the center, realign the covariance — to the RVE/CHE sandwich covariance as an explicit calibration target, so this bridge from calibrated-Bayes philosophy to dependent-effect meta-analysis is this review’s contribution (CL-P3-09, POSITIONING, PRIMARY; G-01, G-23, G-24, G-27). This is the positioning counterpart of the verbatim negative-existence claim, which is single-hosted at §3.5 of the previous chapter and is not restated here. The principle is prior art (Little (2012); Rubin (1984)); Savitsky & Williams remain survey-scoped; and the recent near-neighbors realign toward composite-likelihood Godambe sandwiches under licenses other than Little–Rubin. The strength of the positioning rests, as the negative does, on a documented systematic absence and is phrased conditionally; it is never escalated to a claim that no such method could exist.

The discipline that makes the positioning precise is the combinatorial-gap discipline: every individual ingredient of the conjunction already exists in print, so the gap can only be the unoccupied conjunction, never priority over any single ingredient (G-27). Each near-neighbor instantiates exactly one ingredient (posterior recoloring toward some sandwich in a non-meta-analytic setting) and stops there. Müller (2013) recolors a posterior toward the sandwich in misspecified econometric and time-series likelihoods, preserving the center and realigning the covariance, but never in meta-analysis. Shaby (2014) performs the same move in spatial and extreme-value GLMs, toward a composite-likelihood Godambe target under an asymptotic license and at the mode, not the meta-analytic RVE/CHE sandwich. Frazier, Drovandi and Kohn (2026), Lyddon, Holmes and Walker (2019), and Tanaka (2026) each realign a generalized-Bayes posterior toward a sandwich-flavored target on independent data, in several cases tuning only a scalar, with no meta-analysis and no Little/Rubin license. Lambardi di San Miniato and Sartori (2021) match curvature toward a robust sandwich under composite-likelihood asymptotics, again outside meta-analysis. Luo and Ji (2026) recolor uncertainty for single-dataset functionals; Wang, Lin and Liu (2025) recolor at the MAP under a Shaby open-faced-sandwich license toward a composite-likelihood Godambe target; and Bissiri, Holmes and Walker (2016) supply the enabling Gibbs-Bayes machinery that permits swapping a likelihood target for a loss target without ever performing it for the meta-analytic target. Each is a single ingredient, present in print, and each — by realigning toward some sandwich in some non-meta-analytic setting — confirms rather than occupies the conjunction. The principle, the mechanism, and the substrate are borrowed; the assembly — the calibrated-Bayes license, the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich as the named target, and the dependent-effect meta-analytic setting, all at once — is the unoccupied site this review fills.

That assembly answers why a covariance target may be imported into a Bayesian meta-analysis at all. It does not yet answer why one would want a specifically Bayesian robust variance estimator rather than the frequentist sandwich already in hand. P3 hands forward to P4: why a Bayesian RVE specifically (the predictor-specific diagnostics, the design-effect-ratio exemplar, and the operating-envelope advantages the gap demands over the frequentist all-or-nothing sandwich), with covariance-matching (exact, algebraic) and coverage (asymptotic, simulation-shown) held strictly apart throughout.
