1  Introduction & the inferential mismatch

NoteGuardrails honored in this chapter

Defining a Bayesian robust variance estimator for dependent-effect meta-analysis is the work of three literatures, not one. This chapter names the three literatures, the join this review proposes between them, and the roadmap of the funnel the rest of the book descends. The object the funnel tracks — the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance — is introduced here and never leaves: every chapter hands the same target to the next.

1.1 1.1 The three-literatures mismatch

The problem this book addresses is an inferential mismatch. Defining such an estimator is the work of three literatures rather than one, each supplying one necessary but individually insufficient component (CL-P0-01, HEURISTIC; G-24). Naming the mismatch first, rather than any one field, matters because the contribution is a join across fields rather than an improvement within one, and the join is invisible until all three components are named together.

The first component is robust cluster-sandwich estimation, originating with Hedges et al. (2010). This lineage fixes the target: it points a cluster-robust sandwich at the marginal covariance of a dependent effect-size vector through a working model (aggregated or correlated-effects) that may misspecify the within-study correlation, and it secures consistency in the number of studies without requiring the true within-study correlation to be known. The marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance is the object this literature owns, and the object the other two literatures will be asked to host. What this literature does not supply is a posterior: it furnishes a covariance target and an estimator for it, not a Bayesian inferential machine.

The second component is the Bayesian hierarchical and multilevel meta-analysis apparatus. This tradition supplies the modeling machinery: weakly informative heterogeneity priors, observation-level random effects, multivariate and three-level structures, and the posterior as the primary inferential object. Its strength is expressiveness: it can represent within- and between-study dependence hierarchically and preserve observation-level design matrices. What it does not, on its own, supply is a named covariance target. Because the Bayesian analyst keeps the posterior as the primary object, a frequentist sandwich covariance is not a standard reporting quantity in this literature; the modeling apparatus is present, but the target the first literature owns is not among its objectives.

The third component is the calibrated-Bayes philosophy of Little (2012) and Rubin (1984). Here a Bayesian model is judged against frequentist operating characteristics, and the governing move is to preserve the inference center while realigning the covariance toward a frequentist operating-characteristic target. These two works establish the principle as standing prior art. This literature supplies the license (the warrant for importing a frequentist covariance target into Bayesian inference), but it is a general principle rather than a meta-analytic method, and it points at no particular target and no particular application. Crediting the principle to Little and Rubin is a binding discipline (G-24): this review invents neither calibrated Bayes nor the idea of preserving the center while realigning covariance; only the application of that principle to the meta-analytic sandwich target is novel here.

Each component is necessary, and none suffices alone. The first owns a covariance target but offers no posterior; the second owns a posterior but names no covariance target; the third owns the license that would authorize importing the one into the other, yet it is prior art that has not been aimed at the target. The mismatch is therefore structural rather than a matter of any field’s deficiency: three mature literatures supply three parts of a single inferential object, and that object is well-defined only when the parts are assembled at the point where they meet, the covariance.

1.2 1.2 The join this review proposes

The program’s move is to perform that assembly explicitly, carrying the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance as the object held across all three literatures. The frequentist RVE lineage, from Hedges et al. (2010) through Pustejovsky & Chen (2024), made the robust cluster-sandwich the structural standard for dependent-effect meta-analysis, fixing the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance as the target any analogue would have to match; calibrated-Bayes theory (Little, 2012; Rubin, 1984) supplies the intellectual license to import that same sandwich covariance into Bayesian inference as an explicit calibration target (CL-P0-02, POSITIONING; G-23, G-24, G-27). The two streams are real and independently established: the sandwich-as-standard clause is anchored at its origin in Hedges, Tipton and Johnson (2010) and capped by Pustejovsky and Chen (2024), while the license clause rests on Little and Rubin in the survey and official-statistics context where those authors worked. Stated as a movement of one object, the proposal is to take the covariance target the frequentist lineage already owns and, under the calibrated-Bayes license, adopt it as the object a Bayesian posterior is calibrated to match, preserving the posterior mean while realigning its covariance toward that target.

This conjunction has not been occupied, and the claim that it has not is made here conditionally and defended downstream. Across a broad survey of Bayesian meta-analysis, these three literatures have not been joined at the relevant point: no published meta-analysis has pointed the calibrated-Bayes principle at the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance as an explicit calibration target. The wording governs the whole book. The conjunction is unoccupied, not unattainable: each ingredient already exists in print (posterior recoloring toward some sandwich, robust sandwich estimation, and the calibrated-Bayes principle have each been used somewhere), and this review claims no priority over any single one (G-27). Both qualifiers are retained, the RVE/CHE sandwich target and the Little/Rubin license, so that near-neighbors which recolor toward a different sandwich under a different license are not miscounted as occupants. The assertion rests on a documented systematic search, its strength equals the breadth of that search, and it is never escalated to a proof that no such method could exist (G-23). The verbatim negative-existence statement is therefore deferred: it is asserted once, with its full search breadth, where the Bayesian gap is established (Chapter 3), and restated as positioning where the contribution is assembled (Chapter 6). Here the claim is only that the conjunction is open and that this review proposes to occupy it.

The absence admits a non-pejorative reading. It reflects no deficiency in the Bayesian apparatus, only that a posterior calibrated to a marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance has not been a standard object in this literature. What is missing is not Bayesian capacity but the application of an established license to an established target. The principle that licenses the occupation remains Little’s and Rubin’s; the survey-to-Bayes mechanism this review later adapts remains Williams and Savitsky’s; what is new is the application: pointing an established principle, through an adapted mechanism, at a target the frequentist literature already owns (G-24).

1.3 1.3 Roadmap of the funnel

The rest of the book is one descent, tracking the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance through the three literatures in four beats.

  • P1 — RVE owns it (Ch. 2). The frequentist RVE lineage fixed the marginal sandwich covariance as the estimand, disciplined the small-sample regime with named corrections, and shipped the canonical software. This chapter establishes the target the rest of the funnel must match.
  • P2 — Bayesian meta-analysis lacks it (Ch. 3). The Bayesian tradition arrives at that target with ample modeling capacity and no analogue. This chapter documents the gap (an absence of application, not of Bayesian capacity) and hosts the verbatim gap statement.
  • P3 — calibrated-Bayes permits it (Ch. 4). The calibrated-Bayes principle supplies the missing license, the Williams–Savitsky survey-to-Bayes Cholesky mechanism supplies the operation, and this review names the result CCC. This chapter shows the join is permitted and how it is built.
  • P4 — CCC delivers it, with diagnostics (Ch. 5). A specifically Bayesian RVE earns its place through capabilities the frequentist sandwich does not readily afford (flexible heterogeneity priors, observation-level structure, and per-coefficient design-effect-ratio diagnostics), with covariance-matching and coverage held strictly separate.

The two unoccupied conjunctions then close affirmatively in Ch. 6, where the gap, this review’s matching results, and the matching-versus-coverage distinction are assembled into the stated contribution and the claim-type discipline is set out as a reading key; Ch. 7 bounds the contribution, framing its scope as preconditions rather than apologies. The first beat begins now: the next chapter develops only the first conjunct, namely what that owned frequentist standard is and what covariance it targets.
