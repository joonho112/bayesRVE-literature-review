2  The frequentist RVE lineage owns it

NoteGuardrails honored in this chapter
  • G-15 — RVE is asymptotically consistent in m (the number of studies), not k (the number of effect sizes); in small-m meta-analysis (especially m < 8) Type I error inflates unless a named small-sample correction (HTZ, AHZ, CWB, or CR2) is applied. This chapter never asserts that uncorrected RVE controls Type I error in the small-m regime; every adequacy claim names its correction in the same sentence — the spine guard of the chapter.
  • G-03 — Pustejovsky and Chen (2024, Theorem 2) proved the aggregated and correlated-effects likelihoods proportional — equal up to a parameter-free constant κ — never identical; exact identity is reserved downstream for this review’s own unit-weight-boundary result and is never read into the field’s proportionality result.
  • G-04 / G-14 — CR2 (Bell–McCaffrey) is a design-specific comparator: it improves coverage on balanced designs but degrades under severe imbalance or skewed regressors, and its equality conditions hold only under compound-symmetric within-subgroup blocks and constant subgroup size; it is never universal, never covers all designs, and is never the calibration target.

This chapter also carries forward G-05 (when moderators vary within study it is the aggregated representation that ceases to exist, never the correlated-effects model) and G-25 (the Godambe / estimating-equation substrate motivates the need for a correction; it does not supply one).

This chapter establishes the first leg of the funnel: the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance that any Bayesian analogue must match is a settled, owned frequentist standard. It is owned not by a single paper but by a lineage: one apparatus that matured, link by link, from Hedges, Tipton and Johnson’s origin through Tipton’s small-sample discipline and Pustejovsky and Tipton’s working-model consolidation to Pustejovsky and Chen’s integrative capstone. The chronology carries the argument. Each link extends rather than displaces the one before it, so that what arrives at the end is the completion of a single standard rather than a competing estimator. The object that lineage owns is a specific covariance estimand, the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance of the effect-size vector, and the conditions under which each of its properties holds are stated with care below, because it is that estimand, with those conditions, that any Bayesian analogue must measure itself against. The seven sections trace that maturation: what the lineage made the field-default, what its sandwich targets, why the target is robust, how it disciplined the small-sample regime, why CR2 is a comparator and not the target, which working model survives where the other breaks, and the degrees-of-freedom tuning and canonical software that instantiate the whole apparatus. This chapter renders the standard; it does not re-prove it.

2.1 2.1 The established field-default

Robust variance estimation is the established frequentist field-default for meta-analysis with dependent effect sizes, built from the cluster-sandwich and refined across the lineage from Hedges, Tipton and Johnson (2010) through Tipton (2015; 2015), Pustejovsky and Tipton (2022), to Pustejovsky and Chen (2024); its field-default status is documented both in the methodological lineage and in the practitioner-tutorial literature (Tanner-Smith et al., 2016) (CL-P1-01, POSITIONING). This is a framing claim about the structure of the frequentist literature, not a program theorem: the status is carried as POSITIONING, and the origin paper’s own theorem is held at the field level, never re-elevated.

Hedges et al. (2010) is the origin: it establishes the cluster-sandwich as the frequentist apparatus for dependent-effect meta-regression, furnishing both a correlated/observation-level working model and the cluster-level residual-cross-product sandwich that estimates the marginal covariance of the effect-size vector. Tipton then disciplined the small-sample regime that the origin result left asymptotic (Tipton, 2015) and extended the standard to multiple-contrast tests (Tipton & Pustejovsky, 2015), each move extending rather than overturning Hedges, Tipton and Johnson. Pustejovsky and Tipton (2022) consolidated the working-model choices — correlated-effects, hierarchical-effects, and their combination — into a single deployable framework, and Pustejovsky and Chen (2024) integrated the apparatus into one treatment of aggregated and correlated-effects representations, supplying the analytic equivalence and design-class results this chapter leans on. The status is documented from two directions at once. The methodological lineage names and extends the chain: the more recent multivariate and cross-domain entries (Chen & Pustejovsky, 2025; Welz et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) inherit the same sandwich without overturning it, and Pustejovsky (2025) names the same lineage as the “well-developed and readily available” robustness strand for dependent effects. From the other direction, the practitioner tutorials (Tanner-Smith et al., 2016; Tanner-Smith & Tipton, 2014) and applied syntheses (Cameron & Miller, 2015; Gnambs & Schroeders, 2023) document cross-disciplinary adoption across education, psychology, health, and the microeconometric clustering literature from which the cluster-sandwich also descends, and the canonical software lineage (Fernández-Castilla et al., 2026; Joshi et al., 2022; Williams et al., 2025) carries it into routine use.

The field-default claim is made honestly, with its adoption qualifier intact and stated as a precondition rather than a concession. Tipton, Pustejovsky and Ahmadi (2019) record that RVE was endorsed in principle among the consensus-recommended modeling alternatives for dependent effects, yet appeared in only about five percent of syntheses as of 2016. “Field-default” therefore denotes methodological consensus and emerging adoption (the standard the field recognizes as correct for dependent effects, the one the methods literature converged on and the tutorials teach) rather than universal practitioner uptake. The qualifier sharpens the lineage claim rather than weakening it. The review measures a Bayesian analogue against the estimand this lineage fixed, an object whose authority rests on the lineage’s structural coherence, not on a head-count of applied syntheses.

2.2 2.2 What the sandwich targets

The lineage having fixed which apparatus is standard, the next link fixes what that apparatus targets. The RVE sandwich targets the marginal covariance of the effect-size vector through a working model — aggregated or correlated-effects — that may misspecify the within-study correlation, and is asymptotically consistent in m, the number of studies, without requiring the true within-study correlation to be known (CL-P1-02, CONDITIONAL; G-03, G-15). Pustejovsky and Chen (2024) state the property directly: the sandwich does not rely on the assumed dependence structure being correctly specified, and it requires a large number of independent clusters. The covariance the sandwich estimates is the marginal covariance — the object that survives integrating over whatever within-study correlation the working model posits — and naming it as marginal is a standing discipline of this book. Two preconditions on that consistency are load-bearing and must be kept distinct.

The consistency runs in m (studies), not in k (effect sizes). The estimand is a marginal covariance estimated from cluster-level residual cross-products, and the origin result is explicitly contingent on regularity conditions that bite only as the number of independent studies grows (Hedges et al., 2010; Pustejovsky & Tipton, 2022). A synthesis with twelve studies reporting ten effects each has k = 120 rows in its data frame but m = 12 independent clusters; it is the twelve that the asymptotics count. The cross-domain analogues confirm the same direction under the clusters↔︎studies mapping: econometric clustering theory makes consistency contingent on the number of clusters diverging, not the number of observations (Cameron & Miller, 2015), and the survey design-based sandwich is consistent in the survey central-limit sense, in the number of sampled units (Binder, 1983), with clusters and strata playing the role of studies, never effect sizes. Eliding m into k would misstate the asymptotic regime, and it is precisely the regime where the small-m discipline of §2.4 becomes binding (G-15).

What Pustejovsky and Chen establish about the two working-model likelihoods is a proportionality, not an identity. Their Theorem 2 shows the aggregated and correlated-effects likelihoods are proportional — equal up to a parameter-free constant κ, and not identical (G-03). The distinction is load-bearing for the entire book: proportionality up to a constant suffices for the maximizing arguments and the sandwich target to agree, which is what licenses moving between the two representations, but it is strictly weaker than a likelihood identity. Exact identity is a separate, stronger statement reserved downstream for this review’s own unit-weight-boundary result; it must never be read back into the field’s proportionality theorem. The cross-domain analogues confirm the weaker, correct reading: econometric clustering (Cameron & Miller, 2015) and survey design-based estimation (Binder, 1983) each affirm consistency in the number of clusters, and neither asserts a likelihood identity. What the sandwich targets, then, is a single marginal covariance, reached through either of two proportionally related working models, consistent as studies accumulate.

2.3 2.3 Why the sandwich is robust

Why the marginal sandwich is robust is the link that explains why the lineage’s target is worth calibrating to at all. The sandwich estimator remains consistent under essentially arbitrary misspecification of the within-study correlation structure (which is almost never known in meta-analytic practice), provided the working model’s marginal moments are calibrated to the data-generating process (Cameron & Miller, 2015; Hong et al., 2018; Pustejovsky & Tipton, 2022) (CL-P1-03, CONDITIONAL; G-25). This is the sandwich’s central virtue: its validity does not turn on getting the dependence structure right, only on the marginal moments being approximately correct. Hong et al. (2018) states the rationale plainly: the estimator is asymptotically correct even when the working likelihood is incorrect, and the consolidating lineage (Pustejovsky & Tipton, 2022) carries it as an asymptotic-in-m property of the field. The robustness is asymptotic and conditional on calibrated marginal moments; it is not unconditional, and the prose states the condition with the claim.

The deeper rationale is supplied by the estimating-equation and Godambe-information machinery, of which the design-based sandwich of Binder (1983) is the survey-side origin. That substrate gives the asymptotic justification for why covariance recalibration under working-model misspecification is principled rather than ad hoc: when an estimator solves an estimating equation whose score is correct in expectation, its sampling covariance takes the Godambe sandwich form regardless of whether the full working likelihood is correct, given correctly specified low-order margins. This is the precise sense in which the substrate motivates the need for a correction — it establishes that a marginal covariance target is well-defined and that realigning toward it is licensed by estimating-equation theory — without itself supplying a finite-sample correction (G-25). The literature carries it as motivation, never as proof that a finite-sample correction already exists (Joshi et al., 2022). This misspecification-robustness, asymptotic, conditional, and principled, is what makes the marginal sandwich covariance an attractive calibration target for a Bayesian analogue: it is the covariance an estimating-equation argument says inference should report under misspecification. It is cited here as why-recalibration-is-needed framing, the hinge the calibrated-Bayes pillar (P3) later inherits, not as evidence the correction has already been built.

2.4 2.4 The small-m discipline

The robustness of §2.3 is asymptotic, and the asymptotics bite slowly, which is why the lineage’s most consequential refinement was the small-sample discipline. In small-m meta-analysis, especially m below eight, RVE inflates Type I error unless a named small-sample correction is applied: HTZ, AHZ, CWB, or CR2 (Bell–McCaffrey; Imbens & Kolesár (2016)); uncorrected RVE does not control Type I error when studies are few (CL-P1-04, EMPIRICAL; G-15). This is the chapter’s spine guard, and it is stated qualitatively by discipline: uncorrected small-m RVE over-rejects, and level control recovers only after a named correction. No bare coverage percentage is asserted, and no sentence claims small-m adequacy without naming the specific adjustment that delivers it.

Tipton (2015) is the primary named source. Tipton showed by simulation that uncorrected robust inference over-rejects badly in small-m meta-regression and that level control recovers only via the bias-reduced residual corrections together with Satterthwaite degrees of freedom — the named HTZ-type adjustment. Each subsequent link in the lineage named its own correction, again extending the discipline rather than replacing it. Tipton & Pustejovsky (2015) extended it to multiple-contrast tests with the AHZ correction, showing uncorrected multiple-contrast RVE liberal even at large m. Joshi et al. (2022) carried the cluster wild bootstrap (CWB) as a named small-m alternative, directly stating that small-m RVE inflates Type I error and is controlled only through a named correction. Yang et al. (2024) confirmed that the bare cluster-robust estimator is downwardly biased and fails Type I control unless the number of independent studies is large or a named correction is applied. Beneath this meta-analytic lineage sits its methodological origin: Bell & McCaffrey (2002) derived the bias-reduced linearization later named CR2 and showed by simulation that uncorrected small-sample linearization inflates Type I error, and Pustejovsky & Tipton (2018) implemented CR2 with Satterthwaite/AHT degrees of freedom and documented the over-rejection of the uncorrected Wald test.

The same discipline is corroborated, in its own idiom, by adjacent settings, and the boundary between the four endorsed corrections and these analogues is held explicit. The econometric clustering literature from which the cluster-sandwich descends finds that few-cluster Wald tests over-reject unless a named correction — the cluster jackknife CV3 with a t(G−1) reference (MacKinnon et al., 2023, 2025) or the Bell–McCaffrey degrees-of-freedom adjustment (Cameron & Miller, 2015) — is applied; small-k scalar-summary intervals undercover unless an adjusted reference distribution is used (Zejnullahi & Hedges, 2024); the general-meta-analytic precursor literature on liberal small-sample tests names its own remedy, the Hartung q-variance estimator with a t reference (Hartung & Knapp, 2001); and the multilevel literature warns that ignoring the within-study sampling covariance underestimates standard errors and inflates Type I error (Van Den Noortgate et al., 2015). These cross-domain corrections are corroboration that the small-sample failure and its named remedies are general; the CV3 jackknife and the t(G−1) reference in particular are kept clearly labeled as econometric analogues, not as one of the four meta-analytic corrections the review endorses under G-15. What a working meta-analyst must take from this section is a rule, not a number: in the small-m regime, RVE adequacy is a claim about RVE-plus-a-named-correction, never about bare RVE. An adequacy claim that does not name its correction is not a claim the test supports.

2.5 2.5 CR2 is a comparator, not the target

Among the named corrections, CR2 warrants particular care, because it is the comparator most easily mistaken for a universal fix. The CR2 correction (Bell–McCaffrey; Imbens & Kolesár (2016)) improves coverage on balanced designs but degrades under severe imbalance or skewed regressors, and its compound-symmetric and constant-subgroup-size conditions are not always met (CL-P1-05, CONDITIONAL; G-04, G-14). Even at its canonical development the correction is not universal: Imbens & Kolesár (2016) show that Bell–McCaffrey is nearly inert on balanced designs, helps most under skew or imbalance, yet itself undercovers under skewed errors at one extreme and is conservative at the other. The underlying bias-reduced linearization is exactly unbiased only under independent and identically distributed errors (Bell & McCaffrey, 2002), so the equality it promises is a design-specific property, not a guarantee that holds across designs.

The conditions under which CR2 achieves its equivalence are pinned by the lineage’s capstone. Pustejovsky and Chen (2024, Theorem 3) establish that the CR2-type equivalence holds only under compound-symmetric within-subgroup blocks and constant subgroup size — conditions the paper characterizes as more restrictive and artificial, and which fail in Pustejovsky and Chen’s own worked examples (G-04). Where those conditions break, so does the equality. The degradation is documented across designs: coverage falls materially below nominal in small-k multivariate meta-regression (Welz et al., 2023), and most starkly in crossed phylogenetic designs, where CR2 is inapplicable and cluster-robust inference falls below nominal regardless (Williams et al., 2025). Because CR2’s accuracy is contingent on design balance, it cannot serve as ground truth; the prose never describes it as universal, as covering all designs, or as eliminating Type I error (G-14). CR2 is therefore a design-specific tuning and an external comparator — useful where its design conditions hold, degraded where they do not — never a universal fix and never the calibration target. This is the distinction the chapter carries forward: the object a Bayesian analogue must match is the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance itself, not CR2 treated as ground truth. CR2 is one design-aware way the frequentist lineage approximates that covariance in finite samples; it is not the covariance.

2.6 2.6 The design-class distinction

The lineage also fixes which working model survives when the data structure is most demanding, a distinction this review later inherits as the warrant for observation-level Bayesian structure. Dependent effects are handled by either the aggregated (one-row-per-study) or the correlated-effects (observation-level) working model; when moderators vary within study, the aggregated representation ceases to exist, whereas the correlated-effects model remains well-defined with the sandwich applied at the observation level (Pustejovsky & Chen, 2024) (CL-P1-06, CONDITIONAL; G-05). The mechanism is plain: the aggregated one-row-per-study representation requires study-level predictors, so when a moderator takes different values across the effect sizes within the same study, there is no single study-level row that can represent it, and the aggregated representation ceases to correspond to any model. The correlated-effects model, working at the level of the individual effect size, retains the full within-study design matrix and simply keeps the rows.

The direction of this result is locked (G-05): it is aggregation that breaks, and the correlated-effects model that survives, never the reverse. Inverting it (claiming the correlated-effects model fails) would misstate the entire design-class result and quietly undercut the observation-level structure this review carries into its Bayesian analogue. The “ceases to exist” result itself is sourced uniquely to Pustejovsky and Chen (2024), the capstone of the lineage; the earlier links and the parallel literatures furnish the two representations and corroborate the direction without asserting the analytic result themselves. Hedges et al. (2010) supplies both the correlated/observation-level working model and the synthetic aggregated alternative but does not state the direction, which is Pustejovsky and Chen’s contribution; and the multilevel route reaches the same design-class distinction from the hierarchical side, with Van Den Noortgate et al. (2015) showing that aggregating effect sizes loses information and can make moderator estimation impossible while the observation-level model survives, the same direction, never inverted. For a practitioner with within-study moderator variation, the operational consequence is direct: the observation-level correlated-effects model is not merely the more flexible choice but the only well-defined one. This survival of observation-level structure is the analytic warrant for the Bayesian observation-level model this review carries into P4: a Bayesian analogue that operates at the observation level is calibrating to a target that remains well-defined precisely where the aggregated alternative cannot.

2.7 2.7 Satterthwaite df tuning and canonical tooling

Operating the sandwich in finite samples requires a reference distribution, and the lineage’s routine device for setting it is the last refinement to trace, together with the software that instantiates the whole apparatus. The Satterthwaite degrees-of-freedom approximation is routinely applied in frequentist RVE meta-regression to set the effective degrees of freedom for tests and confidence intervals; it is asymptotically justified but biased under severe imbalance or small m, becoming unreliable once the effective degrees of freedom fall below roughly four (Pustejovsky & Tipton, 2018; Tipton, 2015) (CL-P1-07, CONDITIONAL; G-15). Here too the chronology is one of refinement: the origin paper (Hedges et al., 2010) supplied only the crude m − p correction; the coefficient-by-coefficient Satterthwaite approximation, and the finding that the effective df are covariate-dependent and often far below m − 1, are established by Tipton (2015) and Pustejovsky & Tipton (2018), with Joshi et al. (2022) corroborating both the routine use and the small-m below-nominal behavior. The practical rule that falls out, familiar from any clubSandwich or robumeta warning, is to distrust a test whose effective df drop below about four, because the Satterthwaite reference is no longer reliable there. This df-below-four fragility is exactly the kind of finite-sample imperfection in the field-default tooling whose own limits under small m and imbalance are part of the standing small-sample discipline (G-15), and it ties directly back to §2.4.

That entire apparatus — sandwich, named corrections, Satterthwaite df — is, finally, instantiated in canonical software, which is what carries the standard from the methods literature into a practitioner’s analysis (CL-P1-08, POSITIONING). The clubSandwich package (Pustejovsky and Tipton) and robumeta (Fisher and Tipton) are the canonical implementations of frequentist RVE with small-sample corrections (CR2, HTZ/AHZ, and Satterthwaite degrees of freedom) and the field-standard tooling for dependent-effect meta-analysis (Joshi et al., 2022; Pustejovsky & Tipton, 2018; Tanner-Smith et al., 2016; Welz et al., 2023). Pustejovsky and Tipton (2018) are the methods source that introduces and implements CR2 with AHT degrees of freedom in clubSandwich; Joshi and colleagues (2022) and Tanner-Smith and Tipton (2016) name and use both clubSandwich and robumeta as the standard implementations actually deployed; Welz et al. (2023) names clubSandwich as the canonical software for cluster-robust estimation with small-sample corrections; and the field-tooling record (Fernández-Castilla et al., 2026; Tipton et al., 2019), with Pustejovsky and Tipton (2022), situates them in the broader practitioner ecosystem. The lineage thus closes the loop a practitioner cares about: a fixed estimand, a disciplined small-sample regime with named corrections, a monitored reference distribution, and shipped software that does all three from a single deployable toolchain.

Taken together, CL-P1-01 through CL-P1-08 establish a complete, instantiated, software-backed standard, and they fix a single object: a marginal cluster-sandwich, consistent in m under working-model misspecification, robust under calibrated marginal moments, proportional-not-identical across its two working-model representations, requiring a named small-sample correction at low m, with CR2 as a design-specific comparator rather than the target, the aggregated representation that breaks where the correlated-effects model survives, the Satterthwaite tuning with its df-below-four fragility, and clubSandwich and robumeta as canonical tooling. This is the owned frequentist standard — one apparatus matured into a field-default through the lineage from Hedges, Tipton and Johnson to Pustejovsky and Chen. The marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance it targets is precisely the covariance target a Bayesian analogue would have to match, which is the question P2 takes up: across a broad survey of Bayesian meta-analysis, is there any published method that adopts this sandwich covariance as an explicit calibration target?
