6.1 6.1 Claim-type discipline as a reading key

Every load-bearing claim in this book carries a type, and the type is not decoration — it is a contract about evidence. The taxonomy is the key the reader has been using, often tacitly, across all five preceding chapters; stating it plainly here lets the convergence that follows be read precisely rather than impressionistically. A reviewer can read each tag as an instruction for what to demand. A THEOREM is an exact established result of the program, and of this review only; it is cited motivationally with its source and scope, never re-proved here. A result of the frequentist RVE field is therefore never typed THEOREM but rather CONDITIONAL, POSITIONING, or EMPIRICAL, because the label tracks ownership, not mere mathematical truth. A GAP-ASSERTION is a negative claim with warrant in documented systematic absence across an exhaustive search, never a single citation; phrased as “we found no published method that…,” never as an unconditional non-existence proof. A CONDITIONAL binds a claim to its condition in the same breath — “under [stated condition], [claim]” — and is never read as universal. A POSITIONING claim frames a contribution or a relation, asserted directly but with each component attributed to its owner. An EMPIRICAL claim rests on data or simulation, and its source must be named; bare figures are banned, and a qualitative reading (“near nominal”) stands in until an exact number is verified against its source.

Two rules ride on top of the taxonomy, and together they are what make the funnel auditable. The first is the name-the-target rule: wherever the book touches calibration, a sandwich, or RVE covariance, it names the specific object (the marginal CR2/RVE sandwich covariance for the fixed-effect block, the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\), the conditional versus the marginal covariance) because the argument moves between frequentist and Bayesian idioms and an unnamed “sandwich” invites misreading. The second is the matching-versus-coverage separation: covariance-matching (exact, finite-sample, algebraic) and coverage (asymptotic in \(J\), simulation-shown) are different types of claim living in different sentences wherever both appear, ensuring an algebraic identity is never quietly upgraded into an operating-characteristic guarantee. The calibrated-Bayes principle that licenses the whole construction is itself credited as prior art, not as a program invention, every time it is named. The payoff of the key is traceability: because each claim wears its type, its source, and (where the principle is borrowed) its attribution, a reader can stop at any sentence and reconstruct what would have to be true for it to hold and who would have to be cited to support it. The convergence of §6.2 and §6.3 is legible only because every element arrives pre-typed; §6.4 applies the key exhaustively to the finished contribution.
