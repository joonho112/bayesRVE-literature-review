5  Why Bayesian RVE specifically

NoteGuardrails binding this chapter

This chapter makes the affirmative case for a specifically Bayesian robust variance estimator, and it does so under one overriding discipline: every advantage named here is a positioning claim (a capability the Bayesian paradigm affords), never a claim about how any interval behaves. The frequentist sandwich is the standard this review calibrates to, not a strawman; the contrast is a neutral design-class difference, not a deficiency. Two guardrails dominate, and they are stated first.

  • G-09 (covariance-matching is not coverage, the single most drift-prone discipline in this book). Covariance-matching is an exact, finite-sample, algebraic property of the calibrated posterior’s covariance relative to a named RVE sandwich target. Coverage is a separate operating characteristic — asymptotic in \(J\), simulation-shown, near nominal. The two live in separate sentences throughout; “matches exactly” is never permitted to slide into “guarantees” or “ensures” coverage, and no bare coverage or Type-I percentage appears — only the qualitative “near nominal.”
  • G-22 (the design-effect-ratio diagnostic is survey-scope; its meta-analytic extension is a forthcoming companion paper). The per-coefficient design-effect-ratio (DER) diagnostic is established for Bayesian survey models (Lee 2026, arXiv:2603.07791). The meta-analytic per-coefficient extension to dependent effects is developed in a forthcoming companion paper, not here. Current bayesRVE outputs already expose scoped DER-adjacent quantities; these are narrower than a forthcoming companion paper’s full workflow, never absent.
  • The three capabilities are positioning, not coverage guarantees. Flexible heterogeneity priors, observation-level structure, and per-coefficient diagnostics are modeling capabilities the Bayesian paradigm affords; none is a statement about the operating characteristics of any estimator. The contrast with the frequentist all-or-nothing sandwich is a neutral design-class difference, never a deficiency or a strawman.
  • G-16 / G-17 / G-18 (the Theorem scopes are distinct preconditions, not limitations). The unit-weight boundary is the exact contribution, not a restriction adopted for tractability (G-16); the conditional identity holds over the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\), never arbitrary positive-definite \(V_j\) (G-17); the marginal identity is strictly more general, resting only on a matched marginal Gaussian kernel (G-18). The two Theorem scopes are never flattened to one.
  • G-12 (correct-specification neutrality is conditional on \(\omega^2\)). The identity-under-correct-specification result holds given the plug-in \(\omega^2\) / within-study correlation; if \(\omega^2\) is badly misspecified the target itself is wrong, and no data-driven \(\omega^2\)-misspecification correction is claimed.
  • G-13 (the simulation evidence is proof-of-concept, not universal). The coverage evidence (TSL15 / sim3) is operating-characteristic evidence under specified data-generating processes; multivariate coefficient-level behavior remains open. No simulation figure is cited — “near nominal” only.
  • G-05 (aggregation ceases; the observation-level model survives). When moderators vary within study it is the aggregated (one-row-per-study) representation that ceases to exist; the correlated-effects / observation-level model remains well-defined. The direction is never inverted.

The formal theorems are cited motivationally only and are never re-proved here: they appear as prose cross-references to companion manuscripts (in preparation), not as @-citations, because those manuscripts carry no bibliography entries. The second verbatim, locked negative-existence sentence is single-hosted at §5.6 and appears exactly once; its Chapter 6 appearance is a positioning reprise that never re-asserts the negative.

The previous chapter answered why a covariance target may be imported into a Bayesian meta-analysis at all: the calibrated-Bayes license (Little/Rubin), the survey-scoped Cholesky mechanism (Williams & Savitsky), and this review’s exact unit-weight-boundary identity together vacate the gap that the substrate opens. What they do not yet answer is why one would want a specifically Bayesian robust variance estimator rather than the frequentist sandwich already in hand. This chapter answers that question. The answer is deliberately not “because it covers better.” A Bayesian RVE earns its place by supplying three modeling capabilities the frequentist construction does not readily afford: flexible prior modeling of between-study heterogeneity, preservation of observation-level multilevel structure where aggregation fails, and per-coefficient design-effect-ratio diagnostics. Each is stated below as positioning (what the paradigm can do) and held rigorously from sliding into a statement about how an interval behaves. The chapter then records this review’s exact covariance-matching results, cited motivationally, and closes by stating, once and verbatim, the second gap this review occupies. Throughout, the load-bearing object is named each time it appears (the marginal RVE/CHE sandwich covariance), and the frequentist contrast is held neutral: a difference of design class, never a defect.

5.1 5.1 The three capabilities, as positioning (not coverage guarantees)

The case opens with the three capabilities themselves, framed at the outset as what the Bayesian paradigm affords. A specifically Bayesian formulation of robust variance estimation offers three things frequentist RVE does not readily provide: flexible prior modeling of between-study heterogeneity, preservation of observation-level multilevel structure, and predictor-specific (per-coefficient) diagnostics. These are positioning advantages, not coverage guarantees, each a modeling capability rather than a claim about the operating characteristics of any estimator (CL-P4-01, POSITIONING). The first two capabilities are well-attested in the adjacent literature. Williams, Rast and Bürkner (2018) demonstrate the prior-modeling flexibility a Bayesian heterogeneity model affords: weakly informative and structured priors on the between-study variance, with the heterogeneity parameter treated as a first-class modeled quantity rather than a plug-in. Van den Noortgate, López-López, Marín-Martínez and Sánchez-Meca (2015) and Fernández-Castilla, Jamshidi, Declercq, Beretvas, Onghena and Van den Noortgate (2021) supply the observation-level multilevel structure, in which the within-study design matrix is preserved at the level of the individual effect size. These three carriers establish advantages (i) and (ii), and only those two: none pairs the structure with an RVE/CHE sandwich-calibration target, and none is credited with the diagnostic advantage (iii), which is carried separately below.

The evidentiary posture here is exemplary motivation, not demonstrated superiority, and the bar is exactly that. No on-scope, dependent-effect Bayesian meta-analytic carrier instantiates all three advantages at once; the first two are carried by near-neighbor exemplars from the adjacent survey and small-area-estimation literature, where Rao and Molina (2015) document that flexible Bayesian small-area methods and unit-level multilevel structure are a mature, well-established toolkit. The third advantage, the per-coefficient diagnostic, is the design-effect-ratio diagnostic, survey-scope in origin (Lee (2026b), arXiv:2603.07791; cross-referenced to §5.3) and developed for meta-analytic dependent effects in a forthcoming companion paper, not here. Each near-neighbor instantiates at most two of the three advantages, never in a dependent-effect meta-analytic frame paired with a sandwich-calibration target, and that is precisely the bar the corpus is asked to meet.

One of the three, prior flexibility, also opens a forward door the review acknowledges but does not walk through. Flexible Bayesian prior modeling of heterogeneity is a natural launchpad for Bayesian network-meta-analysis extensions, a downstream use case this review acknowledges but does not undertake (CL-P4-02, CONDITIONAL). The pointer is deliberately conditional: the network-meta-analysis development lies outside this review, no coverage or finite-sample property is attached to it, and the topically nearest corpus entries operate by the wrong mechanism (Wang, Lin and Liu (2025) recolor toward a composite-likelihood Godambe target under a non-informative-prior Open-Faced-Sandwich license, and Liu and colleagues (2025) work frequentially), so neither is read as a supporting carrier. The launchpad is named as a possibility, not promised as a result.

5.2 5.2 Observation-level structure where aggregation fails

The second capability is the Bayesian realization of a design-class distinction the frequentist chapter already established, and the direction of that distinction is load-bearing. The unaggregated, observation-level Bayesian likelihood preserves within-study design matrices even when moderators vary within study (Van Den Noortgate et al., 2015): the regime in which the frequentist aggregated, one-row-per-study representation ceases to exist while the correlated-effects model remains well-defined (Pustejovsky and Chen 2024, cited motivation) (CL-P4-03, CONDITIONAL; G-05). The direction is held fixed: it is the aggregated representation that breaks, not the observation-level model. This is the same design-class distinction fixed for frequentist RVE in Chapter 2 (CL-P1-06), now realized in a Bayesian likelihood: the unaggregated likelihood survives precisely where aggregation cannot express the within-study structure.

The corpus corroborates the structural half of this claim across both survey and frequentist meta-analytic neighbors, while the directional half rests on cited analytic motivation. Van den Noortgate et al. (2015) supply the one-row-per-effect-size observation-level likelihood that keeps the within-study design matrix intact, though in a frequentist REML frame and paired with a single equicorrelation variance component rather than a sandwich-calibration target. Song et al. (2020) exhibit a genuine aggregation-failure mode: a non-linear combined outcome cannot be expressed at the aggregated study level, so observation-level reconstruction is required to represent it at all. Neither carrier states the directional result that the aggregated representation is the side that ceases to exist; that direction is Pustejovsky and Chen’s (2024) analytic distinction, cited motivationally, not a corpus finding.

The within-study working model that makes this structure operational is not re-derived here; it is borrowed as substrate from the calibrated-Bayes chapter. The CCC calibration carried into this regime is blockwise by design (a \(\beta\)-block Cholesky on the marginal sandwich target and a \(\theta\)-block diagonal correlated-effects rescaling), so the observation-level structure is calibrated without attempting a full-joint Cholesky on a structurally rank-deficient sandwich (reprise of CL-P3-08, primary at §4.6; G-10, G-11). And the within-study correlation that working model carries is \(\rho\), the conditional residual Level-1 working correlation of \(y_j\) given \((\beta, \theta_j)\), not the marginal intra-class correlation, which is \(\tau^2\)-inflated through \(\Sigma_j(\tau^2) = V_j + \tau^2\,\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top\) (reprise of CL-P3-10, primary at §4.6; G-19, G-20). Both objects are used here as the ground on which observation-level structure stands; their full statement lives at §4.6 and is not re-hosted. What this section adds is only that the observation-level likelihood is the regime in which that working model has something to preserve — the within-study design matrix the aggregated representation cannot hold.

5.3 5.3 Per-coefficient DER diagnostics: selective vs all-or-nothing

The third capability is the one that most sharply distinguishes a Bayesian RVE, and it is the diagnostic the chapter foregrounds. A per-coefficient design-effect-ratio diagnostic identifies which coefficients require variance correction, enabling selective, predictor-specific correction rather than the all-or-nothing application that the frequentist sandwich provides (CL-P4-04, POSITIONING; G-22). The contrast is a design-class difference, framed neutrally: the frequentist sandwich applies one robust covariance to the whole coefficient vector, by construction; a per-coefficient ratio resolves the correction to the coefficient, telling the analyst that, say, the intercept is well-served by the model-based posterior while a moderator slope is materially under-dispersed. This is an added resolution the Bayesian construction affords, not a deficiency it exposes in the sandwich.

The diagnostic’s origin must be named precisely, because the line between its established scope and its future scope is exactly the chapter’s G-22 discipline. The design-effect-ratio diagnostic is established for Bayesian survey models by Lee (2026b), where a per-coefficient ratio of the design-corrected sandwich variance to the MCMC posterior variance identifies which coefficients are survey-sensitive and need correction. Its meta-analytic, per-coefficient extension to dependent effects is developed in a forthcoming companion paper, not anticipated here: Lee (2026b) performs no meta-analysis, and the dependent-effect version is the forward contribution this review motivates rather than pre-empts. The diagnostic is therefore cited as a survey-scope origin and exemplar, never as a meta-analytic result already in hand.

Two bounded near-neighbors fix what the diagnostic is by showing what it is not, and neither is a meta-analytic DER. Giordano and Broderick (2024) are the named diagnostic, non-corrective neighbor: they compute a frequentist sandwich variance from a single MCMC run, with per-parameter inconsistency governed diagnostically by how tightly each parameter’s posterior concentrates. They show only that Bayesian draws can yield Bayesian-versus-frequentist per-parameter variance comparisons, not a per-coefficient design-effect ratio for dependent meta-analytic effects. Van Lissa, van Erp and Clapper (2023), in Bayesian regularized meta-regression, supply the per-coefficient moderator-selection ingredient by coefficient-magnitude shrinkage (LASSO/horseshoe priors with per-coefficient inclusion), which selects moderators coefficient-by-coefficient but is not a robust-versus-model variance ratio and so does not flag which coefficients need variance correction. The classical multivariate design-effect lineage positions the diagnostic within an established tradition without anticipating it: Hornby et al. (2023) and Williams, McGuire and Savitsky (2025) cast the \(R_2^{-1} R_1\) projection as a parameter-specific design effect in the Kish sense, a survey design effect rather than the meta-analytic per-coefficient ratio. Every carrier is survey-scoped, non-corrective, or magnitude-selection; the meta-analytic per-coefficient DER is, by design, a forthcoming companion paper’s territory.

5.4 5.4 The MCMC-vs-CCC comparison as a forward-pointing diagnostic

The diagnostic acquires its meta-analytic shape as a comparison the review motivates and explicitly hands forward. The MCMC-versus-CCC design-effect-ratio comparison is a data-driven diagnostic for identifying correction-sensitive parameters in meta-analytic dependent-effect inference; this review motivates the diagnostic, drawing on the Bayesian survey-scope design-effect-ratio of Lee (2026b), and points to a forthcoming companion paper, which develops it for dependent effects (CL-P4-05, POSITIONING; G-22). The single survey-scope precedent (Lee (2026b), contrasting the MCMC posterior variance against a design-corrected sandwich target) establishes the comparison as a data-driven diagnostic for correction-sensitive parameters, but performs no meta-analysis; the dependent-effect version is forward-pointing positioning the review motivates without pre-empting. The diagnostic asserts no finite-sample or coverage property of its own; it is a reading of where the two covariances diverge.

What already exists in this review’s software must be stated accurately, because the deferral to a forthcoming companion paper is a deferral of a workflow, not an admission that the diagnostic machinery is missing. Current bayesRVE outputs already include scoped DER-adjacent quantities (DER_mu for the intercept, the \(\theta\) summaries, the CCC scale factors r_k, and the CHE diagonal DER_emp), so the diagnostic ingredient is present and computed, not absent. These outputs are narrower than a forthcoming companion paper’s full contrast-level / default DER workflow for pre-specified contrasts with named variance targets: they are intercept- and diagonal-scoped diagnostics, where a forthcoming companion paper develops the general per-contrast, per-coefficient reporting workflow. The accurate statement is therefore one of scope, not of absence — the current outputs expose a restricted DER-adjacent diagnostic, and the full meta-analytic per-coefficient development is a forthcoming companion paper’s.

5.5 5.5 The program’s matching results (motivational) and matching is not coverage

This is the chapter’s hinge, and it states two things in a fixed order: first what this review proves exactly, then what it does not. The three results below are cited motivationally and are never re-proved here; they appear as prose cross-references to the companion manuscripts, with their numbering pinned exactly so that the conditional bridge, the marginal identity, and the correct-specification result are never conflated.

The first result is the conditional bridge. A companion manuscript, Theorem 1 (the Clusterwise Likelihood Identity), establishes that, at the unit-weight boundary under the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\), the meta-analytic and survey interpretations assign the same clusterwise conditional Gaussian likelihood, hence the same likelihood-derived scores and curvature (CL-P4-07, THEOREM; G-16, G-17). The Cholesky-affine covariance-matching step that reports the target RVE sandwich is supplied by a companion technical report, Theorem 2, together with the companion manuscript’s Corollary 1. The unit-weight boundary is the exact theoretical result and this review’s contribution, not a limitation imposed for tractability (G-16), and the scope is the matched equicorrelation family \(V_j = a_j C_j(\rho)\) — never arbitrary heteroscedastic or arbitrary positive-definite \(V_j\) (G-17).

The second result is strictly more general. A companion manuscript, Theorem 2 (the Marginal Fixed-Effect Sandwich Identity), establishes that, under unit weights and the matched translation, the meta-analytic and survey interpretations have the same marginal fixed-effect sandwich target after integrating out the random intercepts \(\theta_j\) (CL-P4-08, THEOREM; G-18). This marginal argument is strictly more general than the conditional / equicorrelation bridge because it rests on equality of matched marginal Gaussian densities (\(\ell_{\text{surv}} = \ell_{\text{meta}} + c\)) with no compound-symmetric requirement; the affine posterior recoloring to that target is the companion manuscript’s Corollary 1 and the generic covariance-matching algebra of the companion technical report, Theorem 2. The two scopes are not flattened: the marginal identity dominates the conditional one in generality, and the asymmetry is preserved. The third result is a neutrality check. A companion technical report, Theorem 3 (Identity Under Correct Specification), establishes that when the MCMC posterior covariance already equals the target covariance, CCC is the identity transformation: the correction is neutral when the working model is right (CL-P4-09, THEOREM; G-12). This neutrality holds conditional on the plug-in \(\omega^2\) and the within-study correlation; if \(\omega^2\) is badly misspecified the target itself is wrong, and no data-driven \(\omega^2\)-misspecification correction is provided. (The companion manuscript’s Corollary 1 is the recoloring result, not this neutrality theorem, and the two are not interchanged.)

Now the spine of the chapter, stated in two sentences that are never bridged. Covariance-matching, as established by the three CCC theorems above, is an exact finite-sample algebraic property of the calibrated posterior’s covariance relative to the named marginal RVE/CHE sandwich target (CL-P4-10, EMPIRICAL; G-09, G-13). Coverage is a separate operating characteristic: it is asymptotic in \(J\) and demonstrated only by simulation under specified data-generating processes (program simulation evidence, TSL15 / sim3), where the calibrated intervals are near nominal. The two claims are distinct. Exact algebraic matching does not by itself imply nominal interval coverage, and “matches exactly” is never permitted to slide into “ensures coverage.” The simulation evidence is proof-of-concept under the conditions studied, not universal; multivariate coefficient-level behavior remains open, and no specific coverage percentage is cited (G-13). What the theorems give is an algebraic identity; what the simulation gives is an operating characteristic; the contribution is that the first holds exactly and the second is shown, separately, to be near nominal.

5.6 5.6 The second gap, stated verbatim

All three ingredients of a Bayesian RVE with per-coefficient diagnostics now exist in print (MCMC-based Bayesian inference, the design-effect-ratio diagnostic, and the dependent-effect meta-analytic setting), yet the documented synthesis-level sweep across the Bayesian meta-analysis, design-effect / small-area, and Bayesian survey literatures finds the conjunction of all three unoccupied. The gap is therefore combinatorial, and it is stated in this review’s locked wording, reached through the breadth of that search rather than from any single citation (CL-P4-06, GAP-ASSERTION, PRIMARY HOST; G-22, G-23, G-27):

We found no published method that combines MCMC-based Bayesian inference with per-coefficient design-effect-ratio diagnostics for meta-analytic dependent effect sizes.

The strength of the assertion equals the strength of the documented search behind it; it is never escalated to “no such method exists” or to a priority claim over any single ingredient. Every individual ingredient already exists in print (MCMC-based Bayesian inference, design-effect ratios, per-coefficient diagnostics), but the conjunction is unoccupied for the meta-analytic dependent-effect setting. The named near-neighbors are non-counterexamples that sharpen the conjunction rather than close it, and none may be tagged as supporting it. Lee (2026a) instantiates the Cholesky-sandwich-plus-per-coefficient-DER conjunction, but only at survey scope. Lee (2026b) supplies the per-coefficient DER ingredient for Bayesian survey models, with the word “meta-analysis” absent. Van Lissa, van Erp and Clapper (2023) supply per-coefficient moderator selection by coefficient-magnitude shrinkage, not a robust-versus-model variance ratio. Frazier, Drovandi and Kohn (2026) provide neither a robust-versus-model variance ratio nor per-coefficient targeting. Each instantiates one ingredient and stops there; the conjunction — MCMC-based inference, a per-coefficient DER diagnostic, and the dependent-effect meta-analytic setting, all at once — is the unoccupied site, and the meta-analytic per-coefficient diagnostic that would fill it remains the scope of a forthcoming companion paper.

Taken together, the four pillars now close the funnel. P1 fixed the frequentist RVE standard and the design-class distinction; P2 documented the absence of a published Bayesian RVE analogue targeting the RVE/CHE sandwich; P3 supplied the calibrated-Bayes license that authorizes recoloring a posterior toward that sandwich; and P4 has shown what a specifically Bayesian RVE adds (flexible heterogeneity priors, observation-level structure where aggregation fails, and per-coefficient DER diagnostics the frequentist construction does not readily afford) as positioning advantages, with covariance-matching (exact, algebraic) and coverage (asymptotic, simulation-shown) held strictly apart. These threads now hand to the consolidated gap-and-contribution synthesis, where the unoccupied conjunction, this review’s matching results, and the matching-versus-coverage distinction are assembled into this review’s stated contribution and its deferral of the meta-analytic per-coefficient diagnostic to a forthcoming companion paper.
